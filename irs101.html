Business vs Hobby Test

The IRS considers you a business if you show a profit motive. Key factors include:

  • Keep detailed records (receipts, mileage, inventory)
  • Spend substantial time on the activity (e.g., 10+ hrs/week)
  • Depend on income from reselling
  • Show profit in 3 of the last 5 years
  • Have business cards, a separate bank account, or an LLC

Key: A business can deduct losses against other income (like a W-2 job); a hobby cannot.

The Tax Formula

Revenue - COGS - Expenses = Taxable Income

Lowering your taxable income is the key to paying less in taxes. Here's what each part means:

  • Revenue: All your sales, including any shipping fees you charged to the customer.
  • COGS (Cost of Goods Sold): The original cost of the specific items that you actually sold during the year.
  • Expenses: Everything else you spent to run your business, like fees, supplies, and mileage.

Self-Employment (SE) Tax

As a sole proprietor, you'll pay SE tax (for Social Security & Medicare) via Schedule SE. The good news is you get to deduct half of your SE tax automatically.

A) Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

Your biggest deduction. This includes:

  • Purchase price of items that sold
  • Shipping you paid to acquire items
  • Sales tax on inventory purchases
  • Auction fees to acquire inventory
COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases - Ending Inventory

‚ö†Ô∏è Only deduct the cost of an item when it's SOLD, not when you purchase it!

B) Marketplace & Payment Fees

This includes eBay/Poshmark/Mercari/Amazon selling fees, final value fees, and payment processing fees (PayPal, Stripe, etc.). These are 100% deductible.

C) Shipping & Fulfillment

Postage, labels, carrier charges; mailers, boxes, tape, scales, label printers; storage bins, shelving; FBA/3PL fees.

D) Home Office Deduction

E) Vehicle & Sourcing Travel

2025 Standard Rate: $0.67 per business mile. Keep a mileage log!

What Counts:

  • Sourcing trips (thrift, garage sales, stores)
  • Post office/UPS/FedEx runs
  • Bank deposits & supply runs
  • Meeting buyers/picking up purchases

Travel & Meals: For out-of-town sourcing/events, lodging and transport are generally deductible; meals are 50% deductible.

‚ùå Not Deductible: Commuting from home to a regular job or personal errands.

F) Phones, Internet, & Software

Deduct the business share of mobile phone, hotspot, and internet bills. Also includes bookkeeping tools, listing software, photo apps, eBay store fees, and sourcing data tools. Remember to allocate a business % for any mixed-use services.

G) Advertising & Customer Acquisition

Promoted listings, sponsored ads, social media ads, business cards, and photography equipment (backdrops/lighting) used to market your listings.

H) Professional Services & Banking

Fees for your CPA/bookkeeper, legal advice, payment processors, business bank accounts, and a PO box.

I) Equipment & Improvements

Cameras, lights, computers, printers, shelving, thermal labelers, worktables, etc. For items you also use personally, deduct only the business-use percentage.

De Minimis Safe Harbor: This election lets you expense items up to $2,500 per invoice/item immediately instead of depreciating them over several years.

J) Education & Dues

Courses, books, and subscriptions that maintain or improve skills for your *current* business are deductible. You cannot deduct education for starting a new line of work.

K) Insurance & Licenses

Business liability/umbrella add-ons, marketplace insurance add-ons, reseller permits, and business licenses.

L) Retirement & Health Deductions

These are powerful "above-the-line" deductions that don't go on Schedule C but can significantly reduce your taxes.

  • Self-employed health insurance deduction (Form 7206) if you qualify.
  • SEP-IRA / Solo 401(k) contributions (see Publication 560).
  • QBI (199A) deduction: Up to 20% of qualified business income for many sole proprietors.

The Five Buckets of Proof

Keep these records (digital is fine) for at least 3 years:

  1. Income Trail: Marketplace payout statements, 1099-Ks, bank deposits, and a sales log.
  2. COGS Evidence: Receipts, invoices, auction sheets, screenshots for cash buys, plus an inventory spreadsheet.
  3. Expense Receipts: Records showing who, what, when, where, why, and the amount for all expenses.
  4. Mixed-Use Allocations: A written method for how you split phone, internet, auto, and home office costs, with calculations saved annually.
  5. Retention: Keep records for 3 years in general, but 7 years for major assets or bad debt.

Record Keeping Tips

  • Mileage Log: Capture date, start/end miles, destination, and business purpose. Apps are great for this.
  • Home Office: Draw a simple diagram with square footage and take photos to prove exclusive use. Save all utility bills and rent/mortgage documents.
  • De Minimis Safe Harbor: If you use it, attach the one-page election to your tax return and keep invoices showing the unit price was ‚â§ $2,500.

Red Flags & Common Pitfalls

Avoid these common mistakes that can trigger an audit:

  • Claiming 100% business use for a vehicle.
  • Having losses every single year (may be classified as a hobby).
  • Using round numbers for expenses (e.g., $500, $1000).
  • Deducting the personal portion of mixed expenses.
  • Deducting inventory you took for personal use or as gifts.
  • Deducting commuting miles between home and a regular workplace.

Year-End Tax Strategies

  • December Purchases: Buy supplies and equipment before Dec 31 for a current-year deduction.
  • Prepay Expenses: Pay for January expenses like software, rent, or insurance in December.
  • Delay Income: If you're having a very profitable year, consider holding shipments until January.
  • Accelerate Income: If you're having a loss year, push to make more sales before year-end.

Advanced Strategies

  • ¬ß179 / Bonus Depreciation: For expensing higher-cost equipment immediately.
  • Method Changes (Form 3115): Required if you switch your inventory or accounting treatment.
  • Retirement Maximization: Choosing between a Solo 401(k) vs a SEP-IRA depending on your profit margins.
  • S-Corp Election: Can save on SE tax if your profits are consistently over $60k, but adds complexity and cost.

When to Get Professional Help

Consider hiring a CPA when:

  • Your profit exceeds $25,000 per year.
  • You are forming an LLC or S-Corp.
  • You have sales in multiple states.
  • You are hiring employees or contractors.
  • You receive any notices from the IRS.

A CPA might cost $500-$1,500, but their advice could save you $2,000-$10,000 in taxes.

A Note on State Sales Tax

Good news! Marketplace facilitators like eBay, Amazon, and Mercari are now required to collect and remit sales tax on your behalf in most states. This means you are generally only responsible for paying federal and state *income tax* on your profits.
